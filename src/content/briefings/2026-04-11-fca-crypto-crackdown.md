The FCA's announcement on Wednesday of tighter rules around alternative trading venues is the most significant fintech regulation shift in 18 months. What sounds like technical plumbing is actually a watershed.

The regulator is effectively saying: no more fast-tracking of crypto and quasi-financial listings. Any venue seeking to list novel asset classes will now face a standard-setting process that can take 12–18 months. The old Model 3 pathway—rapid authorisation for alternative investment markets—is being wound down.

## What Changed

- Venues must now submit detailed rulebooks 90 days before launch, not 30
- Novel products (tokenised securities, fractionalised real estate) face mandatory Regulatory Technical Standards review
- The FCA is establishing a "sandbox for venues" whereby new platforms can trial products under enhanced monitoring

In plain English: the Wild West is closing. The days of Kraken, Bitstamp, and other crypto platforms listing on alternative venues without extensive scrutiny are over.

## Implications for UK FinTech

This matters enormously for Wise. The payments platform trades on the LSE, but has always operated in a semi-unregulated space thanks to the EMD exemption (EMD = E-Money Directive). The new rules don't directly touch Wise, but they signal regulatory intent: all financial activity, no matter how novel, will be captured.

Revolut's eventual IPO will be the test case. The company operates as an e-money issuer across Europe. The UK regulator will almost certainly impose domestic conditions around:
- Customer segregation of funds
- Operational resilience standards  
- Consumer protection rules specific to crypto holdings

The bar for UK fintech listings is ratcheting upward. That's healthy for market integrity. It's less healthy for venture returns.

## The Real Winner

Established platforms like Interactive Investor and Freetrade, which have already navigated extensive regulatory review, now have durable moats. New entrants face 18-month regulatory delays before they can dream of going public.

Expect M&A activity in this space to accelerate. Larger platforms will acquire smaller ones, bootstrap their way through regulatory approval, and emerge as the consolidated winners.

This is how regulatory arbitrage collapses. First slowly, then all at once.
