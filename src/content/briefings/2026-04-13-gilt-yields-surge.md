Markets repriced the gilt curve sharply yesterday following the Bank of England's meeting minutes and updated guidance. The sell-off was concentrated in the short end, where two-year yields climbed 18 basis points to 3.87%, reversing three weeks of gradual compression.

## The Policy Picture

Deputy Governor Ben Broadbent's speech last week signalled the MPC remains uncomfortable with the inflation trajectory. Core CPI remains sticky at 3.4%, above the 2% target. While headline inflation has drifted down thanks to the energy shock fade, services inflation—the true concern for policymakers—remains elevated at 3.8%.

The market had priced in a 75% probability of a rate cut by June. That's now revised to 35%. The median expectation on the desk is no moves until September.

## Gilt Curve Dynamics

The 2s10s spread widened 12 basis points, reaching 96bps—the widest since February. This reflects genuine economic uncertainty: financial conditions have tightened, credit spreads have backed up, and the consumer is showing signs of fatigue. PLC borrowing has slowed materially, and mortgage approvals remain subdued.

Long-dated gilts (20-year and beyond) have held up relatively better, suggesting the market is pricing in *eventual* normalisation, not sustained terminal rates at current levels.

## What Matters This Week

- **ONS retail sales** (Thursday): January consumer spending beat expectations; February data will test whether that's the start of a trend.
- **CPI print** (Wednesday): Core measures remain the critical gauge. Another upside surprise would justify the BoE's hawkish bias.
- **FTSE 100 earnings**: Dividend season continues. The market is underweight domestic value plays; any dividend surprises could spark a rotation.

## Market Positioning

Currency markets have responded. GBP/USD rallied 0.8% through the week and is now testing 1.268. Option markets are pricing elevated volatility ahead of employment data on Friday. Retail has been buying cable on the dips—classic crowded positioning.

The sterling strength is a double-edged sword. It helps with inflation anchoring but headwinds domestic exporters. Mid-cap stocks with high UK earnings exposure are trading at widened discounts to the FTSE 100.
