The mid-market has been a graveyard for dealmakers since the pandemic. Debt was expensive, strategic buyers were cautious, and private equity was sitting on record dry powder but struggling to deploy at palatable valuations.

That's changing. Three portfolio companies moved in the past 10 days, signalling that the M&A window is genuinely reopening in the £2bn–£5bn space.

## The Thesis

Arla Foods' acquisition of the UK operations of a regional dairy processor was the quiet one—£380m for a business doing £120m EBITDA. That's a 9.2x multiple. Not cheap, but not out of line for food businesses with scale and distribution.

Workspace Group's move to acquire smaller regional office operators is similarly logical. Post-Covid office occupancy has stabilised at 60–70% in regional hubs. Workspace is consolidating the fragmented end of the market.

Meanwhile, the City remains a prize asset. Central London office rents have recovered to peak levels for the top-tier real estate. But the long-tail of secondary stock is trapped in a valuation purgatory.

## What's Fuelling This?

**Financing conditions are normalising.** Debt is no longer prohibitively expensive. SONIA +350 is achievable for investment-grade borrowers. That's enough to make bolt-on M&A accretive.

**Strategic rationale has clarified.** Post-lockdown, business models have settled. Cost bases have been shrunken and optimised. Buyers now know exactly what synergies look like.

**The FTSE 250 discount persists.** Mid-cap stocks trade at 11x forward earnings vs. 14x for the large-cap. That discount is attracting both strategic and financial buyers.

## Positioning

The theme we're watching is *fragmented consolidators*—small-to-mid-cap platforms with:
- Balance sheet capacity (net debt <2.5x EBITDA)
- Platform acquisition experience
- Margin expansion potential through procurement or operational leverage

Regional house-builders, insurance distribution networks, and niche industrial services are on the radar. Average upside from acquisition premiums is 25–35% for the player that gets bought.

## The Counterargument

Macro headwinds haven't disappeared. If rates stay sticky and the consumer falters, we could see deal flows dry up again fast. But for now, the animal spirits are back.
